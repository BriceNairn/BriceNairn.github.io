When people think about GIS, they often picture maps, spatial analysis or cartography. What is less visible is the infrastructure that makes those systems available to hundreds of users across an organisation.

During my time supporting enterprise GIS platforms, I worked with ArcGIS Enterprise environments hosted across development, testing and production infrastructure. My responsibilities ranged from user administration and licensing through to platform upgrades, automation, troubleshooting and operational support.

A typical GIS platform consists of far more than a single application. In practice, it involves a collection of interconnected systems including ArcGIS Enterprise, Portal, ArcGIS Server, ArcGIS Pro, databases, web applications, authentication services and integrations with external business systems.

One of the most valuable lessons I learned was that successful enterprise systems are not defined by technology alone. Reliability, communication and operational discipline are equally important. When an issue occurs, the technical fix is often only part of the solution. Understanding stakeholders, coordinating with infrastructure teams and managing dependencies across multiple support groups is often what determines how quickly a problem is resolved.

<h4>During this period I was involved in:</h4>

<li>Supporting ArcGIS Enterprise environments across DEV, QAS and PRD environments
<li>User access, licensing and platform administration
<li>GIS application troubleshooting and incident resolution
<li>FME Flow automation support and enhancement
<li>Azure-hosted GIS infrastructure
<li>Platform upgrades and maintenance activities
<li>GIS integrations with external business systems
<li>Cross-team coordination to resolve operational issues and dependencies

<blockquote>
    <p>
        One of the more rewarding aspects of the role was helping establish collaboration between teams that traditionally operated independently. By coordinating a working group across multiple support areas, we were able to resolve more than 30 outstanding dependencies that had been affecting operational delivery.
    </p>
</blockquote>


Enterprise GIS is ultimately about enabling others to do their work. While maps and applications are the visible outcome, a significant amount of effort goes into ensuring those systems remain secure, reliable and available when people need them.

Looking back, the experience strengthened my understanding of enterprise architecture, operational support, automation and stakeholder management. It also reinforced my belief that the best technical solutions combine robust technology with clear communication and strong collaboration.
